# Introduction

**DANGER CLOSE** is a war game built on war stories, not on war itself. Its DNA is action movies, tactical video games, and toy soldiers on the carpet — not lived experience. The designer is not a veteran or a military professional. Everything here comes from a love of the fiction, not a claim to the reality.

What makes those stories compelling is real: loyalty under fire, impossible decisions, plans that shatter on contact, squads that hold when they shouldn't. This game doesn't apologize for finding those things exciting.

The fact that war stories are entertaining to us — that a game like this can exist at all — is itself a product of the culture we live in. That's worth sitting with, even if only for a moment. The things that make great stories at your table have destroyed real lives, real families, real communities. We play with the tension of combat not as tribute to the machinery of war, but because these stories are worth telling on our own terms.

This is not a game for glorifying violence or platforming hateful ideologies. Keep it fictional. Keep it human. And if you're a fascist, this game isn't for you. Go fuck yourself.

## Thematic Inspiration

- **The Imperial Guard (Warhammer 40k)** — Gritty warfare where soldiers are expendable, but heroism still matters. Endless trenches, last stands, victory measured in meters.
- **Halo: ODST / Halo Reach** — Small squads dropped into collapsing battlefields. Urban ruins, desperate extractions, and the weight of being just one team in a much larger war.
- **XCOM 2** — Tactical momentum, squad wipes, and the tension of every decision. Losing ground hurts, pushing forward demands sacrifice. Customizing 'your guys', and then losing them heroically/comically.
- **Helldivers 2** — Over-the-top firepower, chaotic missions, and high-stakes extraction under fire. Small squads of highly trained yet still very fragile soldiers on impossible tasks.
- **Clone Troopers (Star Wars)** — Brotherhood forged in battle, mission-first mentality, and individuality within uniformity. A sense of tragic inevitability beneath the armor.

## Vibe

> The advance had been clean — three sectors cleared, minor resistance swept aside like debris. Then the bullets started flying in earnest.
>
> *"Contact front!"*
>
> The squad hit the deck. This wasn't patrol work anymore. This was the kind of fire that killed professionals.
>
> Sergeant weighed the options in the space between muzzle flashes. Flank left through the ruins — risky crossing, good angle. Fall back to better cover — lose ground, keep the squad breathing. Or dig in here and slug it out.
>
> The mission hung on five soldiers and their rifles. On who blinked first.

In DANGER CLOSE, you command a [[Squad]] of [[Trooper|Troopers]] as you advance through hostile [[Sector|Sectors]] toward your [[Objective]], fighting deadly [[Engagement|Engagements]] where positioning and firepower determine who controls the battlefield. Afterwards, you write a Field Report, chronicling the events of the mission.

DANGER CLOSE is a tactical wargame where the roleplaying emerges from the tactics.

During missions, you play to win. Your troopers might have names, personalities, quirks you assigned before the first shot was fired. When your twitchy rookie fails a Mobility Check and gets pinned while the squad pushes forward, you already know why.

But it works the other way too: a trooper you hadn't thought much about keeps surviving impossible odds, and suddenly she's the lucky one, the squad's talisman. You didn't plan that. The dice did.

If you've ever played XCOM or a tabletop wargame and found yourself caring about a soldier not because of a cutscene or the established lore but because of what happened to them across six missions, that's the feeling this game is built around.

And when the roleplaying does feature in missions — one trooper laying down their life to give the others a fighting chance — it hits all the harder.

DANGER CLOSE supports a wide spectrum of tones, from heroic cinematic action to bleak boots-in-the-mud realism. The rules stay the same. Your narration defines the experience. The same [[Advance Roll]] can depict elite supersoldiers sweeping enemy lines or exhausted grunts crawling through mud. Both are valid. Shape the tone that suits your mission.

## Pressure & Pushback

At its core, DANGER CLOSE is a game of pressure and pushback: a tactical tug-of-war across every scale of play.

In the [[Mission Phase]], progress demands constant forward motion, but each new [[Sector]] might contain overwhelming threats that force retreat or hard-fought defense.

Running into heavy opposition then 'zooms in' towards [[Engagement|Engagements]], in which your [[Squad]] must expose themselves to enemy fire in order to gain [[Momentum]] and seize control of a Sector. At the smallest level of detail, we look at the individual actions of [[Trooper|Troopers]] during one round of combat — an [[Exchange]].

And at the largest scale, every Mission contributes to a larger Campaign, where each gain risks provoking an enemy counteroffensive. Whether zoomed in or zoomed out, the game rewards bold advances, but punishes recklessness. You are always deciding: when to push, when to fall back, and how much your Squad can endure.

| Term       | Scope                                                         | Example Impact                 | Timeframe |
| ---------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ | --------- |
| Exchange   | One round of combat within an Engagement                      | Individual trooper tactics     | Moments   |
| Engagement | One firefight to clear a Sector                               | Squad-level positioning        | Hours     |
| Mission    | An effort to achieve an objective involving multiple Sectors  | Tactical success or failure    | Hours     |
| Operation  | Multi-mission plan with clear goal and stakes                 | Pivot point within a campaign  | Days      |

## Getting Ready to Play

### Solo or shared

DANGER CLOSE was designed primarily as a solo game. One player, one squad, one mission. That said, it works well with two players: one making all the tactical decisions, the other handling rules, rolling for the enemy, and setting the scene. With more players, you can split control of individual Troopers within a squad, or run multiple squads on the same mission. You can also run a shared campaign where each player plays their own solo missions: one player defines the front lines and the broader conflict, and individual missions shift the dynamic based on their outcomes.

### What you need

A bunch of d6s, pen and paper. Put on a dramatic soundtrack. Video game OSTs work great for this: Halo, Helldivers, Warhammer 40k, XCOM. Keep a track with battle ambience ready to switch to when [[Engagement|Engagements]] kick off. A single mission tends to take about one to two hours, but you can also break it into individual Engagements and play those bit by bit.

### Journal and Play Aid

Track your squad's story across missions. Squad members, their personalities and relationships, the progress of the larger conflict. As much or as little as you want.

For the missions themselves, I recommend a play sheet: a single page where you track deployed Troopers and their status, the [[Sector]] map and its contents, and [[Momentum]] and positioning during [[Exchange|Exchanges]].

You can do this with pen and paper, or use [opcon.dicegoblin.blog](https://opcon.dicegoblin.blog), an online operations console built for this purpose. There's also a spreadsheet you can copy.

Use simple tokens to represent Momentum and Troopers on your play sheet. They'll shift around a lot.

## Oracle Dice

When the fiction demands an answer and no existing rule covers it, pose a yes/no question where "yes" represents the favorable outcome for the Squad. Roll 1d6:

- For regular 50/50 outcomes, roll {roll:1d6|Oracle}.
- For **Unlikely** outcomes, roll {roll:2d6kl1|Unlikely}.
- For **Likely** outcomes, roll {roll:2d6kh1|Likely}.

| 1d6 | Result                                    |
| --- | ----------------------------------------- |
| 1   | No, and… *(things get worse)*             |
| 2   | No                                        |
| 3   | No, but… *(a silver lining)*              |
| 4   | Yes, but… *(a cost or complication)*      |
| 5   | Yes                                       |
| 6   | Yes, and… *(an unexpected bonus)*         |

Oracle Dice are extremely versatile, and can be used to resolve nearly any situation not covered by the rules — or substitute procedures that don't quite gel with you. For instance:

- The Squad has taken a hidden overwatch position, sniper rifle at the ready. Will any high-ranking targets of opportunity show? *(Unlikely)*
- The Squad's best sniper is taking their shot — will it kill the target? *(Likely)*
- The Royal 3rd Armada has arrived in orbit. Will they be able to push past the blockade and provide much-needed reinforcements? *(Unlikely)*
- The approach to the LZ is through hostile airspace. Will the Squad arrive unscathed? *(Unlikely)*

Use Oracle Dice to resolve uncertain elements during missions: Is the bridge still intact? Did the enemy hear that gunshot? Is there fuel left in the abandoned vehicle? Frame the question, assess the likelihood, and let the dice decide.
